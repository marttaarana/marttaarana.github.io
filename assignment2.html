Investigation · San Francisco Crime Data

Proposition 47
and its Unintended
Consequences

California voters passed Prop 47 in 2014 to reform a justice system that was criminalizing addiction. Drug arrests fell sharply. But the data suggests something else moved in to fill the gap.

Esben Kok · Marta Arana · Sergi Lupon DTU · Social Data Analysis April 2026
Chapter I

A system breaking under its own weight

By 2011, California's prison system had reached a point of constitutional crisis. The U.S. Supreme Court ruled, in Brown v. Plata, that the overcrowding was so severe it constituted cruel and unusual punishment. The state was ordered to release tens of thousands of inmates. Something had to change — not just in the prisons, but in the laws that were filling them.

Much of the intake was being driven by low-level drug possession charges. Under existing California law, simple possession of heroin, methamphetamine, or cocaine was a felony — punishable by up to three years in state prison. Advocates and public health researchers had been arguing for years that the criminal system was the wrong instrument for treating addiction. These were the headlines driving the conversation:

Los Angeles Times · 2011

"Supreme Court orders California to cut prison population by tens of thousands"

The court found unconstitutional levels of crowding — prisons running at 200% capacity — and ordered the state to act.

SF Chronicle · 2013

"California spends $62,000 a year per prisoner — more than Harvard tuition"

Critics pointed out that the same money spent on housing, treatment, and mental health services could break cycles of recidivism.

The Guardian · 2014

"Prop 47: A turning point in California's war on drugs — or a threat to public safety?"

Supporters argued drug addiction is a health crisis. Opponents warned of unintended consequences for property crime.

PPIC · 2014

"California's drug arrest rates are among the highest in the nation"

The Public Policy Institute found Black Californians were arrested for drug possession at rates four times higher than white residents.

California Proposition 47 — The Safe Neighborhoods and Schools Act (2014)

  • Reclassified simple possession of heroin, cocaine, methamphetamine, and other drugs from a felony to a misdemeanor — for personal use amounts only.
  • Extended the same reclassification to petty theft and shoplifting crimes valued at under $950.
  • Applied retroactively: anyone serving a felony sentence for a now-misdemeanor offense could petition for resentencing.
  • Projected savings of $150–250 million per year, to be redirected to mental health services, addiction treatment, and school programs.
  • Passed with 59.6% of the vote on November 4, 2014.

On paper, the logic was sound: treat addiction as a health problem, free up prison beds for violent offenders, and redirect savings toward prevention. The question is whether the data bore that out — and what it missed.

Chapter II

Did it work? Drug arrests fell — fast

The chart below shows the full trajectory of San Francisco's six major crime categories from 2003 to 2017. Two lines tell the Prop 47 story: one going down, one going up. The law did what it intended on drug offenses. But something else was already in motion.

The drop in drug offenses is real, and for many it represents real relief: people no longer incarcerated for carrying a personal-use amount, records cleared, families not split apart by a felony conviction for addiction. The Public Policy Institute of California estimated that in the first year after Prop 47 passed, roughly 4,700 people had their sentences reduced or were released early [3].

The drop in drug arrests is the law working as intended.
What came next is the part nobody wanted to talk about.

But here is what the chart also shows: by the time Prop 47 passed in 2014, larceny was already on a steep upward trajectory that had started in 2012. The law did not create the larceny surge — but it may have accelerated it, and its $950 threshold almost certainly shaped it.

But at what cost?

While drug arrests plummeted, something else was filling the streets — and the data points to the same neighborhoods.

Chapter III

The surge nobody planned for

By 2017, larceny/theft was responsible for more incidents in San Francisco than the next three crime categories combined. This isn't a slight bump — it is a structural shift in the city's crime landscape.

The dominant type of larceny in SF during this period — and the one that defines this era — is theft from vehicles. Smashed windows, stolen bags left on seats, laptop chargers grabbed from back seats. The surge maps almost exactly onto the explosion of tourism and the tech industry's arrival in the city, which brought waves of rental cars full of visible luggage parked in neighborhoods that were not designed for that kind of density.

But there is a second explanation that is harder to dismiss. Prop 47's $950 threshold for petty theft meant that, for the first time, stealing a laptop, a camera, or an iPhone fell below the felony threshold in California. Critics of the law pointed to this as an effective green light for repeat retail theft and vehicle break-ins [5]. Whether or not that is entirely fair, the timing of the larceny surge is at minimum consistent with that argument.

Chapter IV

Same streets, different crime

If larceny were simply a function of population density or tourism, we would expect it to concentrate in the city's busiest areas regardless of Prop 47. But there is a more specific argument to make: that the same neighborhoods that once concentrated drug arrests are now concentrating theft. A change in what the law punishes, not a change in who is committing crimes or where.

Toggle between the two periods on the map below. The pre-Prop 47 drug offense hotspots (2010–2013, in blue) and the post-Prop 47 larceny hotspots (2015–2017, in red) show a striking degree of geographic overlap — especially in the Tenderloin, the Southern district, and the Mission.

The overlap is not perfect — larceny extends further into tourist-heavy areas like Union Square and Fisherman's Wharf where drug offenses were less common. But the core hotspot of the Tenderloin and surrounding districts appears in both maps with unmistakable consistency. People in these neighborhoods were being arrested for drug possession before 2014. After 2014, the same streets were generating larceny reports. The population did not move. The law did.

Methodological note: This geographic comparison is suggestive, not conclusive. Drug offenses and larceny were always present in both periods — what changed is their relative frequency. Correlation in space does not establish that the same individuals shifted from one crime to another. What it does establish is that the neighborhoods most affected by Prop 47 decriminalization were the same neighborhoods that absorbed the larceny increase.
Chapter V

Explore it yourself: crime by district and category

The aggregate city-level trends mask significant variation across San Francisco's ten police districts. The explorer below lets you select any combination of crime categories and filter by district to trace how Prop 47's effects played out differently across the city. Notice, for instance, how the drug decline is sharpest in Tenderloin and Southern, while larceny spikes most dramatically in Central and Northern — the tourist-heavy districts. The Prop 47 marker is fixed in every view so you can always anchor the timeline.

One detail worth pausing on: the Tenderloin district shows an extreme drug offense decline — dropping from over 3,000 incidents per year in 2008 to under 1,000 by 2017. This is one of the most policed and most visible drug markets in California. That such a dramatic numerical drop appears in the data is almost certainly a combination of genuine behavioral change, enforcement reallocation, and reduced reporting of newly-misdemeanor offenses. Separating these three effects from each other is not possible with incident data alone.

Chapter VI

What the data can and cannot prove

The argument this story has assembled — that Prop 47 contributed to the larceny surge by reclassifying petty theft and reshaping enforcement incentives — is supported by the data but not proven by it. This distinction matters.

Crime data is reported crime, not actual crime. The larceny surge coincided with public campaigns urging residents to file vehicle break-in reports, and with the rollout of online reporting tools that made doing so much easier. When reporting is easier, numbers go up even if underlying behavior does not change [4]. The same logic applies to the drug decline: once possession became a misdemeanor, many incidents were likely handled without a formal report at all.

What this data story has shown is correlation and plausibility — a narrative that fits the numbers. A definitive causal argument would require individual-level data linking the same people across time, or a controlled comparison with jurisdictions that did not pass Prop 47. Neither is available in a public incident-level dataset.

That caveat noted: the geographic overlap between pre-Prop 47 drug hotspots and post-Prop 47 larceny hotspots is not something easily explained away. Neither is the timing. Whether Prop 47 was right or wrong on balance — and there are serious arguments on both sides — this dataset suggests the reform did not eliminate crime in the neighborhoods it was most meant to help. It reclassified it.
